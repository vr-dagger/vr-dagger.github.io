Abstract

Concentrate human effort where the policy is least certain

Learning from demonstrations is effective for robotic manipulation, but collecting sufficient task-specific data remains a major bottleneck. Under distribution shift, small errors compound, performance degrades, and expert time is often spent on redundant, low-value corrections instead of the few critical failure cases.

We present VR-DAgger, a human-in-the-loop framework centered on an immersive VR application for dexterous teleoperation, demonstration collection, and selective policy correction. The VR client provides intuitive hand control with synchronized scene visualization, while a backend workstation runs simulation and learning, enabling autonomous rollouts without continuous operator oversight. We use Monte Carlo (MC) dropout to score uncertainty during Isaac Lab rollouts of a diffusion policy and select informative failure segments for correction. These segments are replayed in VR as clips, where the operator selectively labels and corrects the policy's behavior, concentrating supervision where uncertainty is highest — without full-rollout monitoring or a separate intervention classifier.

We evaluate on three dexterous manipulation tasks (Pan pick-and-place, Drawer opening, Valve turning) with a 10-DoF XHand under standard and challenging initial configurations. Active labeling consistently improves over behavioral cloning across all tasks, with gains of up to 22 percentage points. Compared to unguided human-in-the-loop inspection, VR-DAgger reduces per-sample collection time by approximately 40% by focusing review on selected segments rather than full rollouts.

Method

A client–server VR loop that decouples execution from human attention

VR-DAgger follows an iterative loop: a visuomotor diffusion policy is trained on collected demonstrations, rolled out in simulation, and on failure the most uncertain segments are surfaced to a supervisor for correction. Corrective demonstrations are aggregated and training continues.

1

Standalone OpenXR client

A native Unity/OpenXR app on a Meta Quest renders any USD scene locally and syncs it via lightweight object and robot poses — no stereo streaming, so the headset refresh rate is decoupled from server and network speed. Works with any Isaac Lab rigid-body scene.

2

GPU motion retargeting

Tracked human keypoints are mapped to the robot hand by jointly optimizing joint positions and a global SE(3) wrist pose with anchor keypoints, solved in real time on GPU with NVIDIA Warp. Supports dexterous hands through parallel-jaw grippers.

3

Uncertainty-guided correction

MC-dropout scores per-timestep policy uncertainty during autonomous rollouts. The most uncertain failure snippets are replayed in VR for targeted correction — no full-rollout monitoring and no separately trained intervention classifier.

Evaluation Environments

Three dexterous manipulation tasks

All tasks use the 10-DoF XHand and are evaluated under two difficulty levels: Default (standard initial configurations) and Hard (greater variation in object placement and initial hand pose). Crucially, all corrective data is collected only under Default — Hard results measure generalization beyond the demonstrated distribution.

Uncertainty-Guided Active Relabeling

Surface the few snippets that matter

Standard human-in-the-loop methods require the supervisor to watch full rollouts to decide where to intervene. VR-DAgger replaces this with a principled relabeling step that automatically isolates the most uncertain segment of a failed episode.

①

MC-dropout uncertainty

After each rollout we run N=10 stochastic forward passes through the diffusion policy with dropout enabled, and take the mean predictive variance across action dimensions as a scalar uncertainty trace ut over the episode.

②

Top-3 snippet selection

For failed episodes, we select the three peak-uncertainty timesteps and extract a 2 s context window around each, presented as short 2D playback clips inside the VR interface.

③

Targeted correction

The supervisor picks the snippet most in need of correction and takes over teleoperation for the remainder of the episode. Corrective segments are appended and the policy is retrained.

Why it works: on-policy collection naturally surfaces states where the current policy diverges from the expert distribution — states underrepresented in offline demonstrations. Teaching the policy to recover in exactly these states closes the loop that behavioral cloning leaves open, and uncertainty selection focuses the human on those states without watching full rollouts.

Results

Active relabeling consistently beats behavioral cloning

Across all tasks, both Corrective (VR-DAgger) and unguided HIL improve over behavioral cloning, with the largest gains on Hard configurations — even though no corrective data is ever collected under Hard difficulty. Corrective approaches the quality of full human oversight while cutting per-sample collection time by ~40%.
